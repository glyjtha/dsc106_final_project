Methods

We used a publicly available dataset from PhysioNet, collecting data from 10 healthy young male adults. Participants completed cognitive and neuroimaging tests twice: once well-rested, and once after staying awake for 24 hours.

Our analysis focuses on:

  • fNIRS (Functional Near-Infrared Spectroscopy): to detect changes in brain oxygenation, particularly in the frontal lobe, during rest and task conditions.
  • CANTAB cognitive tasks: to measure reaction time, memory, attention, and error rates before and after sleep deprivation.
We processed the raw signals, cleaned the data, and visualized spatial and temporal changes in behavior and brain activity.

  • What will be the most challenging of your project to design and why?
    1. The most challenging part is selecting appropriate statistical methods because of the specialty of our data. 2. Our data focus on cognitive‚Äìbehavioral tests, and it is hard to explain what each task is doing without practical simulation and visualization, so we decided to integrate sample tasks to let users interactively play around. 3. The data itself has flaws in correlation, and a single summary statistic is hard to use to find meaningful relationships; consequently, our initial graphs seem ordinary and lack deeper interaction. 4. For example, we attempted to find correlations between latency and accuracy, but we ultimately obtained nearly horizontal lines, indicating little to no direct correlation. 5. Moreover, the interactive tools are limited because our subjects are homogeneous in demographics, reducing variability. 6. Last but not least, the critical fNIRS component involves human brain functional connectivity, and it is difficult to demonstrate these changes without a 3D brain anatomy graph. 7. However, we are still exploring methods to integrate 3D brain views and enable interactive region-specific functions, which will be our main challenge moving forward.

  • Discussion and Next Steps

    At this stage, our initial findings confirm that even a small, homogeneous sample can reveal measurable effects of sleep deprivation on reaction times and error rates across cognitive tasks. In particular, fNIRS‚Äêderived hemodynamic signals demonstrate consistent increases in prefrontal activation after 24 hours without sleep, suggesting that the brain exerts additional effort to maintain performance. We have found that embedding interactive elements‚Äîsuch as the Cognitive Reaction Game and the sonification feature‚Äîhelps users experience fatigue‚Äôs impact firsthand, making the data more compelling than static charts alone.

    Our design insights indicate that users should be able to toggle seamlessly between ‚Äúbaseline‚Äù and ‚Äúdeprived‚Äù states. The time‚Äêaxis slider and dropdown controls we introduced are a strong foundation, but we plan to enhance them further by adding annotations that explain sudden shifts in metrics. Similarly, the sonification approach requires careful calibration: we will refine pitch and volume changes so they correspond more directly with ŒîHbO magnitudes, allowing users to instantly link auditory cues to cognitive load. Although our current CSS and color palette improve clarity, we will standardize color encoding‚Äîusing red exclusively for declines and green for improvements‚Äîacross all charts and controls to avoid any potential confusion.

    Looking ahead, we plan to expand our participant pool to include females and older adults, which will allow us to examine whether demographic differences produce distinct fatigue profiles and enhance the generalizability of our findings. We also intend to develop a ‚Äúfatigue risk index‚Äù that combines behavioral metrics with fNIRS signals into a single composite score, enabling users to quickly assess overall cognitive strain. Additionally, we will refine interactivity by introducing tooltips that display statistical results (for example, p-values and effect sizes) when users hover over summary charts.

    In conclusion, we have successfully integrated raw data processing, interactive visualizations, and preliminary statistical summaries. The most significant challenges ahead involve selecting robust statistical methods suitable for a small, homogeneous sample and upgrading our fNIRS representations from two-dimensional heatmaps to spatially intuitive three-dimensional brain views. By continuing to refine our visual, auditory, and statistical components, our ultimate goal is to deliver a polished, fully interactive web dashboard that not only presents scientifically accurate findings but also immerses users in the lived experience of sleep deprivation‚Äîunderscoring that sleep is not merely restorative rest but a critical foundation for optimal brain function.
